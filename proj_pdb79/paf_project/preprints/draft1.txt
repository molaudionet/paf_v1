A Wave-Domain Representation for Protein Binding Pocket Embedding
Overview

We introduce Pocket Acoustic Fingerprinting (PAF), a deterministic wave-domain transformation of protein binding pocket structure.

Rather than aggregating residue features directly in spatial domain, PAF maps pocket residues to oscillatory basis functions and derives a fixed-length spectral embedding via Fourier transform.

Method Summary

For each pocket residue:

Radial position defines temporal localization.

Physicochemical features determine base frequency.

A second structural anchor introduces angular frequency modulation.

Amplitude weights encode feature importance.

Residue contributions are summed to generate a time-domain signal.
The magnitude of its Fourier spectrum constitutes the embedding.

Residue features include physicochemical properties, secondary structure, contact density, flexibility, and BLOSUM62 profile (30 dimensions total).

We evaluated PAF on 50 kinase structures across multiple families.

Similarity was measured via cosine similarity of embeddings.

Conceptual Origins: From Spectroscopy to Structural Embedding

The conceptual foundation of Pocket Acoustic Fingerprinting (PAF) is informed by experimental spectroscopy.

In NMR, IR, ESR, and UV–Vis spectroscopy, we do not directly observe atomic coordinates.
Instead, we measure frequency-domain responses that encode structural and electronic information.

Examples:

¹H / ¹³C NMR: Chemical shifts reflect electronic environment and local geometry.

IR spectroscopy: Vibrational frequencies encode bond strengths and functional groups.

ESR: Hyperfine splitting reveals radical intermediates.

Color changes (e.g., solvated electrons in liquid ammonia): Optical absorption encodes electronic structure.

In all cases:

Structural information is expressed as a spectral signature.

The experimenter does not inspect raw spatial coordinates.
They interpret structure through transformations into frequency space.

Spectral Thinking Applied to Proteins

Protein binding pockets are typically analyzed geometrically:

Distances

Graph topology

Surface meshes

Feature histograms

PAF asks:

What happens if we treat a binding pocket as a composite signal and analyze its spectrum instead of its raw geometry?

In spectroscopy:

Position → phase

Electronic environment → frequency

Population → intensity

In PAF:

Radial position → temporal localization

Physicochemical state → base frequency

Angular orientation → frequency modulation

Residue importance → amplitude

This mapping is not literal physical resonance.
It is an information-theoretic analogy inspired by spectral reasoning.

Why This Matters

Spectral transforms have several properties that are appealing for structural biology:

Global aggregation
The Fourier transform captures distributed structure as coherent spectral features.

Interference patterns
Constructive and destructive interference may reveal higher-order organization not obvious in spatial domain.

Compactness
Spectra often summarize complex structure into lower-dimensional signatures.

Stability under small perturbations
Minor geometric noise may average out in frequency space.

These properties motivated exploration of wave-domain encoding for protein pockets.

Distinction from Literal Sonification

PAF is inspired by sonification principles but is not dependent on audible playback.

We do not claim proteins “sound” a certain way.

Instead:

Oscillator superposition is used as a structured aggregation mechanism.

FFT provides a deterministic embedding.

The output is a numerical spectral vector, not an audio file.

This maintains scientific rigor while leveraging the conceptual clarity of spectral reasoning.

Intellectual Integration

The development of PAF is influenced by:

Chemical engineering training in transport and wave phenomena

Spectroscopic analysis of reaction monitoring

Computational modeling and signal processing

Structural chemistry intuition regarding electronic environment

The goal is not to anthropomorphize proteins acoustically,
but to ask whether frequency-domain representations can reveal discriminative structural organization.

Conceptual Basis

The design of Pocket Acoustic Fingerprinting (PAF) is informed by principles underlying experimental spectroscopy. In spectroscopic methods such as NMR, IR, and ESR, structural and electronic information is not interpreted directly from spatial coordinates but from frequency-domain signatures that reflect local environments and collective interactions. Chemical shifts, vibrational modes, and hyperfine splittings encode structure through transformations into spectral space. These methods demonstrate that frequency-domain representations can compactly summarize complex molecular organization.

PAF applies an analogous signal-processing framework to protein binding pockets. Rather than aggregating residue features directly in spatial domain, residues are mapped to localized oscillatory basis functions whose temporal position reflects radial geometry and whose frequency components encode physicochemical properties. Angular relationships are incorporated through frequency modulation relative to a secondary structural anchor. The superposition of these oscillators generates a time-domain signal that is subsequently transformed via Fourier analysis into a fixed-length spectral embedding. This transformation is deterministic and does not depend on supervised training.

Importantly, PAF does not posit physical resonance phenomena in proteins. Instead, it leverages spectral transformation as an information-theoretic aggregation strategy. By mapping distributed structural features into frequency space, the approach aims to capture higher-order organization and interference patterns that may be less apparent in direct geometric aggregation. This spectrally derived embedding provides a compact, parameter-controlled representation of binding pockets that can be evaluated independently of learned neural architectures.
